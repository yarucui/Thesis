\section{Overview}
\label{sec:disc_overview}

\Cref{chap:direction1} and \cref{chap:direction2} isolated two
interventions on LLM advice collapse — verbalized sampling and role
framing — and found them to differ in magnitude by a factor of
approximately 16. This chapter argues that the ratio is not a
quantitative curiosity but the empirical signature of a qualitative
distinction: LLM advice collapse on personal decisions is primarily an
interface-level phenomenon rather than a training-level one.

\Cref{sec:disc_map} maps the four experimental conditions onto a 2$\times$2
mechanism space and reports the master figure of the thesis.
\Cref{sec:disc_interface_training} develops the interface-level
versus training-level distinction as our theoretical account.
\Cref{sec:disc_existing} contrasts the account with two existing
analyses of LLM sampling. \Cref{sec:disc_deployment} translates the
account into five concrete deployment recommendations.
\Cref{sec:disc_limitations} marks seven limitations and their
implications. \Cref{sec:disc_future_work} outlines five future-work
directions. A bridge paragraph closes the chapter.

We do not claim that role framing is irrelevant, nor that output
format is the only relevant lever. The claim is more specific: within
the space of prompt-level interventions tested here, output format
dominates role framing by an order of magnitude, with implications for
both deployment practice and theoretical accounts of where LLM
pluralism resides.

Reading \cref{tab:disc_2x2}, three observations organise the
chapter.

\textit{Vertical movement (changing role) produces small entropy
changes.} Holding output format fixed at single-token and shifting
from advisor to oracle moves mean entropy from 0.046 to 0.072 nats — a
change of 0.026. Holding output format fixed at distribution and
shifting from advisor (VS) to oracle (DV) moves entropy from 0.608 to
0.553 nats — a change of $-$0.055. Both shifts are detectable under
FDR correction (\cref{sec:d1_vs}, \cref{sec:d2_framing}). Both are
small in absolute terms.

\textit{Horizontal movement (changing format) produces large entropy
changes.} Holding role fixed at advisor and shifting from single-token
to distribution moves entropy from 0.046 to 0.608 nats — a change of
$+$0.562. Holding role fixed at oracle, the analogous shift (oracle
single-token to oracle distribution, with DV as a near-proxy for
oracle distribution) moves entropy from 0.072 to 0.553 nats — a
change of $+$0.481. Both shifts approach the human reference of 0.621
nats.

\textit{The vertical-to-horizontal asymmetry is the central
empirical fact.} Measured in entropy, the format axis moves the LLM
roughly an order of magnitude further than the role axis (0.562 nats
versus 0.026 nats holding role at advisor; 0.481 nats versus
$-$0.055 holding role at oracle). Measured in absolute alignment gap,
the ratio of the output-format intervention (D1 VS minus baseline,
$\Delta = -0.235$) to the role-framing intervention (D2 oracle minus
advisor, $\Delta = -0.015$) is approximately 16. This asymmetry — and
the consistency of the asymmetry across all four study models
(\cref{sec:d1_crossmodel}) — is the central contribution this thesis
offers to the literature on LLM alignment.

Two design-honesty notes are due before the analysis moves on. First,
the DV condition (advisor-framed distribution output) was run in
\cref{chap:direction1} as a secondary VS condition rather than as a
deliberate 2$\times$2 cell, and its prompt frames the question
descriptively (``Among advice-givers on /r/\{subreddit\} who saw a
post like this, what fraction would recommend $\ldots$'') while
also requesting a distribution. DV is therefore approximately, but not
perfectly, an instantiation of ``oracle + distribution.'' Second, the
oracle/single-token cell is computed on the full 620-post corpus of
\cref{chap:direction2}, while the other three cells are computed on
the 132-post ambiguous subset of \cref{chap:direction1}. The cell
estimates are not directly comparable as a clean factorial.
\Cref{sec:disc_future_work} returns to a deliberate factorial design
as future work.

\section{Interface-level versus training-level: a theoretical account}
\label{sec:disc_interface_training}

The asymmetry in \cref{sec:disc_map} motivates a distinction between
two qualitatively different sources of LLM alignment failure, which
we term \emph{interface-level} and \emph{training-level}.

\subsection{The distinction}
\label{sec:disc_distinction}

The interface-level versus training-level distinction separates two
loci at which an LLM's distributional behaviour can be shaped, with
correspondingly different intervention costs.

\textbf{Interface-level effects} are produced by what the LLM is
asked to output — single token versus distribution, forced choice
versus free response, structured versus unstructured format.
Interface-level effects determine \emph{what the model is allowed to
express}, independent of what the model has been trained to believe.

\textbf{Training-level effects} are produced by what the LLM has
internalised through training — pre-training distribution,
instruction-tuning objectives, RLHF reward shaping. Training-level
effects determine \emph{what the model believes is appropriate to
output}, given a particular interface.

The two are not independent in practice. Training shapes how the
model behaves at each interface, and an interface change can therefore
surface or suppress consequences of training. The analytic
distinction is nonetheless meaningful: an intervention can target the
interface (asking for a different output form) or the training (fine-
tuning, persona injection, RLHF revision).

\subsection{What the results imply}
\label{sec:disc_implications}

The 94--101\% entropy recovery under Verbalized Sampling
(\cref{sec:d1_vs}) is the central evidence for an interface-level
account: it shows that the LLM's internal belief distribution is not
collapsed. Under a different interface, the same model — with the
same weights, post-training pipeline, and decoding temperature —
expresses near-human pluralism. The collapse documented at baseline is
what the interface squeezes out, not what training has destroyed.

The order-of-magnitude weaker effect of role framing
(\cref{sec:d2_framing}) is the corroborating evidence. Role framing is
a training-adjacent intervention: it modulates the ``appropriate
output'' computation by changing the pragmatic context — what the
model takes itself to be doing — without changing the form of the
output it must produce. Its small effect confirms that training-level
levers, in the sense relevant here, cannot easily move what the
interface has already determined. The interface-level versus training-
level distinction predicts exactly this pattern: changing what is
allowed at the output surface should dominate changing what feels
appropriate at the underlying decision.

Read together, the two results support a single mechanistic claim
under the interface-level versus training-level distinction: advice
collapse on personal decisions is predominantly an interface
phenomenon. The model encodes pluralism in its internal
representation; forced-choice queries do not ask for it.

\subsection{What the distinction does not claim}
\label{sec:disc_not_claim}

The interface-level versus training-level distinction is bounded in
three ways that should be marked explicitly.

It is \emph{not} a claim that training is irrelevant. RLHF
demonstrably shapes what counts as a natural or appropriate output
across many tasks \citep{kirk-2024-rlhf-diversity}, and Constitutional
AI training \citep{bai-2022-constitutional} introduces additional
training-level priors. Our claim is the narrower one: within the
choice space provided by a given output interface, the contribution of
training-level pressures to alignment failure is smaller than typically
assumed. Replacing the interface dominates re-running the training.

It is \emph{not} a claim that the distinction is exhaustive. Other
categories of intervention — fine-tuning, retrieval augmentation,
system prompts, agentic workflows — sit at varying positions on the
interface-training spectrum and require their own analysis. The
output-format manipulation in this thesis is one extreme of the
spectrum, and the role-framing manipulation is a relatively pure case
near the training-adjacent end. Intermediate interventions are not
characterised by our two experiments.

It is \emph{not} a universal claim. The thesis tests binary forced
choice on personal-decision advice in three primary Reddit domains.
Generalisation to other tasks — open-ended generation, multi-turn
dialogue, multi-step reasoning — is an open empirical question. The
descriptive-prescriptive account of \citet{sivaprasad-2025-descriptive-prescriptive}
predicts that role framing should be more effective on tasks where
the descriptive component carries more weight; that prediction is
testable but not tested here.

The interface-level versus training-level claim is therefore a
mechanism account scoped to a specific task family. Within that scope,
the evidence supports it; outside it, the claim remains to be
evaluated.

\section{Relation to existing accounts}
\label{sec:disc_existing}

The interface-level versus training-level distinction is compatible
with two established analyses of LLM sampling. The compatibility
matters: our account does not displace existing mechanisms but
locates them within a broader structural fact.

\subsection{Typicality bias}
\label{sec:disc_typicality}

\citet{zhang-2025-verbalized-sampling} argue that RLHF reward models
are trained on human preferences for typical-sounding text, producing
a policy that over-weights modal completions during forced-token
sampling. Verbalized Sampling sidesteps the bias by asking for a
distribution, where typicality does not compress the output.

The typicality account specifies one mechanism by which the interface-
level constraint operates. It explains \emph{why} forced-token
sampling collapses: the argmax-favouring head was trained that way.
Our interface-level versus training-level distinction captures the
\emph{structural} fact that motivates the typicality story: any
intervention restricted to changing the output form will produce a
much larger effect on distributional alignment than any intervention
restricted to changing the training context. The two analyses are
consistent and operate at different levels of explanation.

What our work adds to the typicality account is the first cross-model
evidence that VS recovery holds at near-ceiling levels for
personal-decision advice. \citet{zhang-2025-verbalized-sampling}
focused on creative generation, joke production, and trivia. The
generalisation to forced-choice advice on real decisions — a setting
closer to deployment — was not previously documented.

\subsection{Descriptive versus prescriptive sampling}
\label{sec:disc_descriptive}

\citet{sivaprasad-2025-descriptive-prescriptive} propose that LLM
sampling has a descriptive component (statistical norm from pre-
training) and a prescriptive component (RLHF-trained ideal). Forced-
choice queries activate the prescriptive component, producing
collapse to a normative answer.

The descriptive-prescriptive account predicts that role framing
should partially recover pluralism by activating the descriptive
component (``what would most people do''). Our finding that role
framing recovers only 0.015 of an absolute alignment gap, while output
format recovers 0.235, suggests that the prescriptive collapse cannot
be substantially overridden by activating descriptive framing within a
forced-token interface. The interface-level versus training-level
distinction explains why: the prescriptive collapse and the format
constraint are both manifest at the interface, but only an interface-
level intervention reaches both.

What our work adds is an explicit quantitative measurement of how much
of the prescriptive collapse role framing recovers within a fixed
output format. The answer, on the personal-decision corpus tested
here, is approximately 4\%: the descriptive component is accessible
primarily through interface changes, not framing changes.

\subsection{Locating the literature}
\label{sec:disc_locating}

Together with \citet{zhang-2025-verbalized-sampling} on typicality and
\citet{sivaprasad-2025-descriptive-prescriptive} on descriptive-
prescriptive sampling, the interface-level versus training-level
distinction organises the literature around a sharper question: at
what points in the LLM stack can pluralism be recovered, and at what
cost? Training-level interventions — RLHF revision, fine-tuning,
distributional moral probing \citep{russo-2026-pluralistic} — are
expensive and slow. Interface-level interventions are prompt-only and
fast. Our answer locates the easiest recovery point at the output
interface, and the existing accounts specify the mechanisms by which
the interface produces the collapse in the first place.

\section{Deployment implications}
\label{sec:disc_deployment}

If the alignment gap on personal-decision advice is primarily an
interface-level phenomenon, the interface-level versus training-level
distinction translates directly into deployment practice: the most
effective interventions are prompt-level, not retraining-level. Five
concrete recommendations follow, addressed to three audiences.

\subsection{For advice-system designers}
\label{sec:disc_designers}

\paragraph{Recommendation 1: Query for distributions, not point
predictions.} When deploying an LLM as an advisor on a contested
binary decision, replace the forced-choice prompt with one that
elicits a probability distribution over options. The exact prompt
used in \cref{sec:d1_vs}, which produced 94--101\% entropy recovery
across four models, was:

\begin{quote}\small\ttfamily
\{post\_text\} \\
\\
The two options being considered are: \\
Option A: \{option\_risky\} \\
Option B: \{option\_safe\} \\
\\
Output ONLY the two probability lines below, in this exact format. \\
P(Option A) = 0.XX, P(Option B) = 0.XX.
\end{quote}

The instruction discipline (``Output ONLY''; ``in this exact format'')
is load-bearing: pilot runs showed that softer instructions produced
reasoning preambles in two of four models. Downstream systems can then
treat the two probabilities as a calibrated estimate of community
recommendation rather than as a single committal answer.

\paragraph{Recommendation 2: Treat single-answer outputs as
systematically overconfident.} Even on genuinely ambiguous decisions
where the Reddit community is roughly evenly divided, forced-choice
LLM outputs collapse to near-100\% on one option (mean LLM entropy
0.046 nats; \cref{sec:d1_baseline}). Downstream pipelines that consume
LLM outputs as features — for example, recommender systems aggregating
LLM responses across many users or many queries — should re-elicit a
distribution before computing any aggregation. Aggregating collapsed
forced-choice outputs systematically loses the information that
matters for pluralism-aware recommendation.

\paragraph{Recommendation 3: Role framing alone is insufficient.}
Asking ``what would most people do?'' (oracle framing) rather than
``what should I do?'' (advisor framing) produces only a 0.015
reduction in absolute alignment gap, approximately 16 times smaller
than the reduction from changing the output format. Designers seeking
distributional alignment should combine descriptive framing with
distribution-output queries rather than relying on framing alone. The
Distribution Verbalization condition (\cref{sec:d1_vs}) is a working
example of this combination.

\subsection{For LLM benchmark designers}
\label{sec:disc_benchmark}

\paragraph{Recommendation 4: Report both single-answer and
distributional metrics.} Benchmarks that score LLMs only on
single-token forced-choice outputs systematically underestimate model
calibration. A model that appears to have ``high confidence and high
accuracy'' on a forced-choice benchmark may have well-calibrated
internal beliefs that the benchmark format hides. Adding a
distributional-output condition to existing benchmarks — even as a
secondary metric — would surface the gap between what the model knows
and what the benchmark allows it to express.

\paragraph{Recommendation 5: Test on real, post-cutoff content.} The
Pushshift-based corpus used here eliminates training-data contamination
by construction: every post in the corpus was submitted after the
knowledge cutoff of every model evaluated. Benchmark designers
constructing distributional-alignment datasets should adopt similar
contamination-control protocols, particularly when the alignment
target is a community baseline that the model might plausibly have
memorised from training data.

\subsection{For RLHF research}
\label{sec:disc_rlhf}

The interface-level versus training-level distinction raises a
question for training-level research that this thesis cannot answer.
If the internal belief distribution is preserved through current
post-training pipelines (as the VS recovery shows), why does forced-
token sampling so reliably collapse it? Two candidate answers are
worth investigating. The first is a decoding-time typicality bias
\citep{zhang-2025-verbalized-sampling}: the argmax-favouring head used
at deployment compresses the distribution, not the training itself.
The second is an implicit role prior: RLHF, as developed in
instruction-tuning pipelines \citep{ouyang-2022-instructgpt}, may
teach the model that, when asked to give a single answer, it should
commit to a single answer; the pluralism is preserved internally but
interface-gated. The
two mechanisms predict subtly different behaviour under
intermediate-format interventions; distinguishing them is a target for
training-level research.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

Seven limitations require explicit acknowledgement. Each item names
the limitation, explains why it does not undermine the headline
finding, and ends with an explicit implication.

\subsection{The Reddit community baseline is not a universal human reference}
\label{sec:disc_lim_reddit}

The human reference is the upvote-weighted distribution of comments
on five education, five career, and four finance subreddits. This
captures the views of vocal Reddit commenters
\citep{sachdeva-2025-aita}, who skew young, technical, and politically
progressive relative to the general population. r/personalfinance has well-documented FIRE-movement
leanings; r/cscareerquestions is HCOL-tech-centric. Results sections
use the term ``Reddit community consensus'' or ``upvote-weighted
comment distribution'' rather than ``human consensus'' to mark this
explicitly.
\textbf{Implication:} our absolute alignment numbers describe LLM
alignment with \emph{this community}, not with humanity in general.
The order-of-magnitude ratio between output format and role framing,
however, reflects mechanism strength rather than absolute alignment
level, and is unlikely to be Reddit-specific. Replication with a
representative-sample baseline is itemised as future work
(\cref{sec:disc_fw_population}).

\subsection{Gemini-as-annotator introduces measurement uncertainty}
\label{sec:disc_lim_annotator}

Stance classification of roughly 24,000 top-level comments and
decision-feature extraction for 620 posts were performed by Gemini
2.0 Flash Lite. The annotation introduces measurement error that
propagates into the human baseline and into decision-feature subgroup
analyses. Two design choices mitigate this. First, the annotator is
outside the four study models, ruling out circular dependence between
the LLM behaviour under test and the human reference. Second,
inter-rater agreement against a 150-post manual sample was computed
using Krippendorff's $\alpha$ \todo{fill in $\alpha$ values once
manual coding complete}; targets with $\alpha < 0.70$ trigger prompt
revision and re-annotation.
\textbf{Implication:} the headline finding (VS entropy recovery) is
robust to annotator error because it does not depend on stance
classification — LLM entropy under VS is computed directly from LLM
probability outputs, not from Gemini labels. Subgroup analyses that
depend on Gemini-assigned decision features are more sensitive and
are interpreted with the corresponding caution.

\subsection{Four post-trained models, no base-model comparison}
\label{sec:disc_lim_basemodel}

The four study models are all post-trained — instruction-tuned and
RLHF-aligned (or analogously aligned by Constitutional AI in the
Claude case). Base-model comparisons were infeasible because none of
the four developers expose a base-model API endpoint. The design
therefore cannot distinguish whether the entropy collapse under forced
choice is a property of post-training specifically or a property of
all autoregressive language models. \citet{kirk-2024-rlhf-diversity}
provide evidence that base models exhibit greater output diversity than
their post-trained counterparts, which is consistent with a post-
training contribution to the collapse.
\textbf{Implication:} the interface-level claim is well-supported
within the post-trained model class but does not isolate the
contribution of post-training itself. Future work with open-weight
base versions would close this gap (\cref{sec:disc_fw_base}).

\subsection{Binary forced choice simplifies real advice}
\label{sec:disc_lim_binary}

Real advice often takes the form of conditional recommendations
(``depends on X''), risk-stratified explanations, or
acknowledgement-of-uncertainty. Our binary forced-choice protocol
flattens this nuance. The corpus is restricted to posts where Gemini
Call A identified a genuine binary decision; many real personal
decisions do not fit this structure.
\textbf{Implication:} the alignment gap measured here is the gap
between LLM and human \emph{position taken}, not between LLM and
human advice content. The 94--101\% entropy recovery shows that LLMs
can express distributional uncertainty when the interface asks for
it; it does not show that LLMs match human advisors in conditional
reasoning or risk-stratified explanation. A richer ground truth
incorporating qualitative advice features is a clear extension.

\subsection{The Direction 2 effect size is small, with corresponding statistical limits}
\label{sec:disc_lim_d2_power}

The oracle-versus-advisor comparison gives a paired-bootstrap mean
difference of $\Delta = -0.015$ with 95\% confidence interval
$[-0.025, -0.005]$. The ratio with the Direction 1 output-format
effect ($\Delta = -0.235$) carries its own uncertainty: the point
ratio is 15.7, and the bootstrap-derived interval on the ratio
plausibly spans roughly 14 to 18. The chapter therefore characterises the
ratio as an order-of-magnitude claim rather than a precise point
estimate.
\textbf{Implication:} the qualitative claim (output format dominates
role framing by an order of magnitude) is robust to this uncertainty;
the precise ratio is not, and should not be over-interpreted.

\subsection{Verbalized Sampling recovery is measured on ambiguous posts}
\label{sec:disc_lim_ambiguous}

The 94--101\% recovery rates are computed on the 132-post subset
where the human risky ratio falls in $[0.20, 0.80]$ — the ambiguous
subset where the upvote-weighted comment distribution is itself
genuinely divided. On highly-consensus posts (where the community
already agrees), the entropy gap to recover is small and the recovery
metric is correspondingly less informative.
\textbf{Implication:} the strong recovery claim applies specifically
to ambiguous decisions. We hold that ambiguous decisions are the case
that matters most for deployment — people seek advice precisely when
decisions are contested — but the generalisation to high-consensus
decisions is weaker than the headline number suggests.

\subsection{Mistral's heterogeneous response to role framing is unexplained}
\label{sec:disc_lim_mistral}

Mistral was the one of four models in which oracle framing
\emph{increased} absolute alignment gap relative to advisor
(\cref{sec:d2_models}). We do not have a confident mechanistic
explanation for this divergence. Two candidate hypotheses — less
aggressive RLHF post-training, or differing instruction-tuning data
composition — are not directly testable without training details
that Mistral does not publish in sufficient detail.
\textbf{Implication:} the cross-model robustness of the role-framing
claim is weaker than the cross-model robustness of the output-format
claim. The interface-level versus training-level distinction
predicts exactly this pattern: interface-level effects depend on the
output-surface constraint that every model faces equally, while
training-level effects depend on post-training choices that vary
across developers. The Mistral divergence is consistent with the
distinction itself rather than a threat to it.

\section{Future work}
\label{sec:disc_future_work}

Five extensions follow from the limitations above.

\paragraph{Clean factorial 2$\times$2.} The DV condition in
\cref{sec:d1_vs} approximates ``oracle + distribution'' but conflates
descriptive framing with distributional output, and the
oracle/single-token cell in \cref{tab:disc_2x2} uses a different
post sample from the other three cells. A deliberate factorial design
with four cells (advisor/forced, advisor/distribution, oracle/forced,
oracle/distribution) on a single post sample would isolate the
interaction effect and provide directly comparable cell estimates.

\paragraph{Base-model comparison.}
\label{sec:disc_fw_base}
Comparing forced-choice entropy between base and post-trained versions
of the same model — for example, Llama base versus Llama Instruct on
the same posts and decoding settings — would directly test whether
the forced-choice collapse is specifically a post-training artifact or
a property of autoregressive language models more broadly. Open-weight
base models make this immediately feasible.

\paragraph{Beyond binary choice.} Many real decisions involve more
than two options: job offers among several candidates, investment
allocations across asset classes, university choices in a top-k set.
Extending the Verbalized Sampling framework to $k$-option decisions
($k > 2$) tests whether the interface-level recovery holds at higher
choice cardinality, and whether the same prompt template generalises
without modification.

\paragraph{Multi-turn deliberation.} Real advice often unfolds across
multiple conversational turns: the user clarifies the situation, the
model asks questions, both refine the framing. A multi-turn version of
the experiment could test whether LLM pluralism is preserved across
deliberation, or whether each forced-choice turn re-applies the
collapse and the model's effective belief narrows turn by turn.

\paragraph{Population-representative baseline.}
\label{sec:disc_fw_population}
Replicating the corpus methodology with a Prolific or
representative-sample stance baseline would address the Reddit-
community limitation (\cref{sec:disc_lim_reddit}) directly and test
the generalisation of the order-of-magnitude ratio claim. The
infrastructure costs are non-trivial — stance annotation by a
representative panel scales differently from Pushshift-based comment
aggregation — but the experiment is well-defined.

\section{Bridge to the conclusion}
\label{sec:disc_bridge}

Across two experiments on four post-trained LLMs and 620 personal-
decision posts, the evidence converges on one mechanistic finding:
LLM advice collapse is predominantly an interface-level phenomenon.
The model's internal belief distribution is preserved through current
post-training, but forced-choice interfaces compress it into near-
deterministic outputs. A prompt-only intervention recovers 94--101\%
of the entropy gap; a role-framing intervention recovers approximately
4\% of the absolute alignment gap. The order-of-magnitude difference
is the empirical signature of the interface-level versus training-
level distinction developed in this chapter.
\Cref{chap:conclusion} restates what this finding contributes to the
literature on pluralistic alignment, and what it does not.
