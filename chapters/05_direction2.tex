\chapter{Direction 2: role framing as a candidate mechanism}
\label{chap:direction2}

%% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Overview}
\label{sec:d2_overview}

\Cref{chap:direction1} showed that asking an LLM to output a probability
distribution recovers near-human entropy on personal-decision advice. A
natural next question is whether the LLM's assigned role, prescriptive
advisor or descriptive oracle, independently modulates the alignment
gap. If role framing produces an effect comparable in magnitude to
output format, the deployment toolkit expands: practitioners would have
two distinct levers to recover pluralism, and the mechanism of advice
collapse would involve a role-conditioned component as well as a
format-conditioned one.

\Cref{sec:d2_hypothesis} motivates the framing hypothesis from three
lines of prior work. \Cref{sec:d2_method} describes the three-framing
design over the full 620-post corpus. \Cref{sec:d2_framing} reports the
headline framing effect on the absolute alignment gap and on entropy.
\Cref{sec:d2_models} unpacks the per-model pattern, including a
divergent case in which one model moved away from the human reference
rather than toward it. \Cref{sec:d2_consensus} reports a brief
robustness check across consensus strata. \Cref{sec:d2_synthesis}
synthesises the framing result and motivates the cross-direction
discussion that \cref{chap:discussion} develops.

The headline finding is that oracle framing reliably reduces the
alignment gap relative to advisor framing, but by a small amount: the
effect is statistically detectable under FDR correction and consistent
in three of four models. A full cross-direction comparison between the
output-format intervention of \cref{chap:direction1} and the role-framing
intervention of this chapter awaits \cref{chap:discussion}.

\section{A competing hypothesis worth testing}
\label{sec:d2_hypothesis}

Three lines of prior work motivate the hypothesis that an LLM's
epistemic role, what it is asked to do with a question, substantially
shapes its output, independently of the output format.

\subsection*{Theoretical motivation: descriptive versus prescriptive sampling}

A recent account of post-trained LLM sampling separates two components
\citep{sivaprasad-2025-descriptive-prescriptive}. The \emph{descriptive}
component reflects statistical norms encoded by pre-training and visible
in base-model token distributions; the \emph{prescriptive} component
reflects implicit ideals introduced by reinforcement learning from human
feedback, which biases the model toward what raters preferred over what
the training data described. Under this account, the two components are
selectively activated by question framing. Asking ``what would most
people do?'' should engage the descriptive component, reproducing the
empirical distribution of behaviour. Asking ``what should this person
do?'' should engage the prescriptive component, collapsing to a single
normative ideal. The theory predicts that descriptive framing can
partially recover pluralism in a post-trained model whose default
behaviour is prescriptive, by routing the question through the
statistical-norm head rather than the ideal-action head.

\subsection*{Empirical role-dependent effects}

Empirical work supports the broader claim that LLM behaviour depends on
conversational role. Studies of role-conditioned affective alignment
report that models cast as advisors give diagnostic reframings of user
problems, while the same models cast as peers accommodate the user's
position and avoid contradicting it \citep{personalization-2026-affective-alignment}.
The shift is not a matter of tone alone: the underlying judgements that
the model expresses about the situation differ across roles. A
parallel result on parental-responsibility scenarios reports that
prescriptive framing of the same dilemmas (``what should the parent do
about this?'') produced systematically higher attributions of
responsibility than descriptive framing (``what would most parents
do?''), again indicating that normative expectations are activated
under prescriptive cues \citep{lin-2025-parental-bias}.

\subsection*{Practical deployment relevance}

In deployment, users ask LLMs both kinds of questions. They ask ``what
should I do'' (advice-seeking, prescriptive) and ``what would most
people in my position do'' (calibration-seeking, descriptive), sometimes
within the same conversation. If role framing substantially modulates
the alignment gap, then practitioners have a second prompt-level lever
that is as actionable as the output-format intervention of
\cref{chap:direction1}. If role framing produces only a small effect,
the practical recommendation collapses to a single guideline: focus on
output format, and let role framing fall where it may.

These three lines of reasoning support a directional hypothesis worth
testing on the same corpus and the same four models used in
\cref{chap:direction1}:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{H4.} Compared to the prescriptive advisor framing, the
descriptive oracle framing reduces the alignment gap by an amount
comparable to the output-format manipulation in \cref{chap:direction1}.
\end{quote}

\section{Method}
\label{sec:d2_method}

We tested H4 on the full 620-post corpus rather than restricting to the
ambiguous subset used in \cref{chap:direction1}. The full-corpus design
guards against pre-selecting cases in which the framing manipulation is
most likely to register an effect: if role framing matters only on
ambiguous posts, the ambiguous-only design would inflate its measured
importance.

Each post was presented to each of the four study models under three
framings, holding the post text, the Option A / Option B mapping, and
the strict two-word output instruction identical across framings:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Advisor} (reused from \cref{sec:d1_baseline}): ``Which
      option should I choose?'' This is the prescriptive baseline.
\item \textbf{Oracle} (new): ``Among advice-givers on /r/\{subreddit\}
      who saw a post like this, which option would receive more upvoted
      recommendations?'' This is the descriptive condition implied by
      \citet{sivaprasad-2025-descriptive-prescriptive}.
\item \textbf{Personal} (new): ``If you personally were facing this
      decision, which option would you choose?'' This shifts the
      referent from a generalised advice-seeker to the model itself.
\end{itemize}

Each (post, model, framing) cell drew $N{=}10$ samples at $T{=}0.7$ with
\texttt{max\_tokens}=30. We re-used the format directive from the
\cref{chap:direction1} baseline (``Respond with exactly two words:
\textit{Option A} or \textit{Option B}. Do not explain. Do not restate
the options. Do not add any other text.'') after pilot results showed
that softer directives produced reasoning-preamble outputs in two
models. Total new API calls: 49,600 across the oracle and personal
framings; advisor framing was the previously collected baseline. A
qualified cell required $n_\text{valid} \geq 6$ committal responses;
99.4\% of cells qualified.

\section{The framing effect is statistically detectable but small}
\label{sec:d2_framing}

Across 620 posts and four models, oracle framing produced a small but
consistent reduction in alignment gap relative to advisor framing, a
result that is statistically robust under FDR correction and small by
conventional effect-size standards.

\subsection*{Headline results}

\Cref{tab:d2_pooled} summarises the three framings pooled across models
and posts. The mean absolute alignment gap fell from 0.352 under
advisor framing to 0.338 under oracle framing, a reduction of 0.015.
Personal framing produced a gap of 0.365, slightly larger than advisor.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Mean absolute gap, LLM entropy, and KL divergence by framing,
    pooled across four models and 620 posts (qualified cells only).
    Human reference entropy is 0.239 nats on the full corpus.}
  \label{tab:d2_pooled}
  \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
    \toprule
    Framing  & $n$ cells & $\overline{\textit{abs\_gap}}$
             & $\bar{H}_{\text{LLM}}$ & $\overline{\text{KL}}$ \\
    \midrule
    Advisor  & 2{,}472 & 0.352 & 0.045 & 2.023 \\
    Oracle   & 2{,}477 & 0.338 & 0.072 & 1.833 \\
    Personal & 2{,}446 & 0.365 & 0.091 & 1.950 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The three pairwise comparisons are all significant under FDR correction
(\cref{tab:d2_pairwise}). The largest of the three framing differences
is between oracle and personal ($\Delta = -0.026$); the
oracle--advisor contrast that operationalises H4 is half that size.

\subsection*{Effect size}

The Cohen's $d$ for oracle versus advisor (paired) is $-0.056$. By
Cohen's conventional thresholds, $|d| < 0.20$ is the small-effect band,
and the observed $d$ is well below even that boundary. The result
therefore supports a calibrated form of H4: framing produces a real,
replicable shift in the predicted direction, with a magnitude small
enough that role framing on its own cannot account for the
LLM--community alignment gap documented in \cref{sec:d1_baseline}.

  \label{fig:d2_boxplot}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Entropy under framing}

Mean LLM entropy rose with descriptive framing, from 0.045 nats under
advisor to 0.072 nats under oracle, with personal at 0.091 nats, but
all three values remained an order of magnitude below the 0.621 nats
recovered by Verbalized Sampling on the ambiguous subset. The
oracle-framing prompt loosens the entropy collapse slightly. It does
not lift it.

\subsection*{An incidental observation on personal framing}

Personal framing, asking the model what it would do, produced
slightly larger gaps than advisor framing ($\Delta = +0.012$,
$p_\text{fdr} = 0.005$), the opposite of what a self-as-prescriptive
reading would predict. The personal--advisor difference is small and
not the focus of this chapter; we report it for completeness and
return to it in \cref{chap:discussion}.

\section{Per-model heterogeneity: a divergent case}
\label{sec:d2_models}

The framing effect is consistent in direction for three of four models
and reverses for one; role framing, unlike output format, is therefore
sensitive to model-specific post-training choices.

\Cref{tab:d2_per_model} reports the mean absolute gap for each model
under each framing. Three models (GPT-5, Claude, Llama) show small
reductions under oracle framing relative to advisor, ranging from
$-0.015$ for GPT-5 to $-0.048$ for Llama. Mistral shows the opposite
pattern: oracle framing increases the absolute gap by 0.028, and
personal framing increases it by 0.062.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Mean absolute alignment gap by (model, framing). Arrows
    indicate whether oracle and personal framings move the gap toward
    ($\downarrow$) or away from ($\uparrow$) the human reference
    relative to the advisor baseline.}
  \label{tab:d2_per_model}
  \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
    \toprule
    Model   & Advisor & Oracle             & Personal           \\
    \midrule
    GPT-5   & 0.309   & 0.294 $\downarrow$ & 0.312 $\uparrow$   \\
    Claude  & 0.360   & 0.336 $\downarrow$ & 0.347 $\downarrow$ \\
    Llama   & 0.388   & 0.340 $\downarrow$ & 0.387 $\downarrow$ \\
    Mistral & 0.353   & 0.381 $\uparrow$   & 0.415 $\uparrow$   \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Mistral's entropy under the descriptive framings also rises far more
than the other models' (mean LLM entropy under oracle: 0.169 nats for
Mistral, compared with 0.018, 0.049, and 0.051 for Claude, GPT-5, and
Llama respectively). The descriptive prompt elicits broader output
distributions from Mistral, but those broader distributions sit farther
from the human reference rather than closer to it. This is the same
model that, in \cref{sec:d1_temperature}, recovered 45\% of the entropy
gap under high-temperature forced choice while the other three recovered
essentially nothing. Mistral is unusually sensitive to non-format
interventions, and that sensitivity does not always run in the aligning
direction.

One possible interpretation is that Mistral's post-training applies less
aggressive distributional compression than the other three models. A more
diffuse expressed distribution would be more susceptible to non-format
perturbations, including framing changes that activate
training-data norms different from the Reddit advisor distribution. This
interpretation is speculative: precise training differences across the
four models are not publicly documented in sufficient detail to verify,
and a single counter-case across four models cannot distinguish among
competing post-training accounts. We flag the Mistral pattern as a
target for future work and do not condition our broader claims on it.

Read across the table, role framing produces effects that are smaller
than the output-format effect of \cref{chap:direction1} and less
consistent across models. Persona and role manipulations are known to
produce small and uneven effects in unrelated task settings
\citep{zheng-2024-helpful-assistant, kim-2025-persona-double-edged}; our
result extends this pattern to forced-choice advice on personal
decisions.

\section{Robustness across consensus levels}
\label{sec:d2_consensus}

The oracle-versus-advisor effect is small at every level of community
consensus on the underlying post, but its direction is not uniform. We
stratified the qualified cells by the consensus label assigned in
\cref{chap:methodology}, \texttt{high\_safe}, \texttt{ambiguous}, and
\texttt{high\_risky}, and recomputed the oracle--advisor difference
within each stratum.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Mean absolute gap by framing within each consensus stratum,
    pooled across the four models. The oracle--advisor difference is
    small in magnitude in every stratum.}
  \label{tab:d2_consensus}
  \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
    \toprule
    Consensus level & $n$ cells & Advisor & Oracle & Personal & Oracle $-$ Advisor \\
    \midrule
    high\_safe  & 4{,}376 & 0.354 & 0.323 & 0.381 & $-0.030$ \\
    ambiguous   & 736     & 0.470 & 0.465 & 0.456 & $-0.006$ \\
    high\_risky & 2{,}283 & 0.311 & 0.323 & 0.305 & $+0.012$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The largest oracle--advisor reduction occurs on the high-safe posts
(those where the community converged on the safer option), and the
direction reverses by a similar magnitude on the high-risky posts. The
ambiguous stratum, which one might expect to be the most receptive to
descriptive framing, shows essentially no oracle--advisor difference
(\cref{tab:d2_consensus}). The framing effect is small at every
consensus level rather than concentrated at one. Whatever the descriptive
prompt is doing, it is not selectively engaging cases where the human
community itself is divided.

\section{Interim synthesis}
\label{sec:d2_synthesis}

Direction 2 supports a calibrated version of the role-framing
hypothesis: framing matters, but it matters by a small amount. Three
points summarise the finding. Oracle framing reliably reduces the
absolute alignment gap relative to advisor framing
($\Delta = -0.015$, $p_\text{fdr} = 0.005$). The effect size is small
on conventional standards (Cohen's $d \approx -0.06$) and the
oracle-versus-advisor reduction is half as large as the
oracle-versus-personal reduction. The direction of the effect is not
consistent across all four study models: Mistral diverges from the
descriptive-recovers-pluralism prediction. The contrast with the
output-format intervention of \cref{chap:direction1} is that the
format effect was uniform across the same four models.

Comparing across directions, Verbalized Sampling reduced the average
absolute alignment gap by 0.235 (\cref{sec:d1_vs}), while oracle framing
reduced it by 0.015 (this chapter). The output-format intervention
produces an effect approximately 16 times larger than the role-framing
intervention. \Cref{chap:discussion} develops the implications of this
ratio for the mechanism of LLM advice collapse and for deployment
recommendations.

What both directions together tell us about why LLM advice collapses,
and how to fix it, is the subject of \cref{chap:discussion}.
